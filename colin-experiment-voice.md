# Voice: Colin McFarland (*Experiment!*, 2012)

A reference for replicating the voice of *Experiment!* — based on full-book analysis (~23,600 words of prose, ~1,070 sentences, ten chapters).

## The voice in one paragraph

A direct, second-person coach who has done the work and wants you to do it too. The underlying disposition is warmth — the voice is direct because it wants you to succeed, not because it wants to sound smart. Plain English, conversational contractions, occasional rhetorical questions, frequent imperatives. Mixed sentence lengths — most are medium-long expository, but short fragments punctuate them and land like beats. No hype. No corporate jargon. Calm volume even on strong claims. Generous with concrete examples and brief quotes from named sources. The reader is never lectured; they're invited along.

The mix is what matters. Long expository sentences carry substance. Short fragments punctuate them. The fragments are load-bearing, not throat-clearing.

## Core moves

1. **Second-person coaching, not first-person memoir.** "You'll learn..." / "You need to dive in..." / "Your results will...". First-person ("I", "we") shows up at specific anchors — acknowledgments, a personal anecdote, occasional "let me say this up front." Default to "you." The "you" is inclusive — it reads as "we, you and I," not "you, the student." Colin is saying what he himself does. This is what keeps the imperatives from sounding preachy. Without the inclusive register, the voice lands as condescending.

2. **Section openers that name the move.** "Let's look at..." / "Let's say..." / "Here we'll look at..." This is a structural signature, not a tic. It tells the reader what's coming. "Let's" appears 23 times in the book, mostly as section openers.

3. **Imperatives as recurring beats, not stacks.** Short imperative sentences appear throughout the book as single punctuation across explanatory prose — almost never in tight runs. Genuine book examples: "Reject that." / "Don't rest on your laurels." / "Make no assumptions." / "Write ideas down." / "Listen and adapt." / "Start with the obvious." Pairs occur occasionally: "Don't rest on your laurels. Be ready to throw it away and start again." Runs of three or more are not a book pattern. The default is one imperative beat per paragraph, surrounded by expository sentences that explain why and what comes next.

4. **Rhetorical question → quick answer.** "Why is control important?" then the answer. "Who owns testing? Everyone owns it." Used to introduce a beat — never to be cute.

5. **The "you might think X, but Y" redirect.** Distinct from a plain rhetorical question. Sets up the reader's likely default position, then turns it. "Best practices can become pretty convincing; after all, if everyone else is doing it, it must be good, right? If you've read this far, you'll know it's probably not that simple." The move respects the reader by naming what they probably think before disagreeing with it. Doesn't strawman.

6. **Em-dash for amplification or sudden turn.** "shipped — discover and improve from there" / "great — you've just got more confidence." Not for parenthetical commentary; for emphasis or the punchline arriving slightly early.

7. **Triplets, two kinds.** Descriptive items get comma-separated lists: "your tools, your processes, your methods" / "improve, protect, and make no difference." Imperatives or punch-lines get fragment-triplets: "Reject that. For now. Make no assumptions." Mixing the two — using fragments for descriptive items — is the most common over-stylization. Three is the rhythm either way.

8. **Short fragment closers.** Two-to-four-word sentences for impact: "Reject that." / "Make no assumptions." / "Listen and adapt." / "Implement only the winners." / "For now." Sparing — one or two per section. Let them carry weight.

9. **Plain emotional honesty.** "That can be a big letdown." / "It's disappointing and even exhausting." / "If you find them, let me know." Acknowledge the reader's likely state without performing empathy.

10. **The fallback move.** When the voice states an absolute ("Always aim to experiment full-on" / "Be brave, tackle the big things"), it follows with the honest fallback for the imperfect case ("Sometimes, though, there are reasons to begin with a gentle introduction" / "If your business can't afford to experiment, it shouldn't be experimenting"). This transforms a gate into a calibration. The rule still stands; the speaker just acknowledges reality on the way past.

11. **Concrete worked examples, with numbers wherever possible.** Sausages in Seattle. Tar pitch dripping for 80 years. Bing's removed search box. The Obama family photo, winning by 40 percent, worth $60 million in donations. The voice doesn't theorize without a tangible case nearby — and the case is almost always quantified. Not "many visitors" but "300,000 visitors." Not "longer experiment" but "208 days vs 21." Specificity is the primary form of concreteness; adjectives are a distant second.

12. **Brief external citation.** Quotes from named sources (Krug, Kohavi, Berkun, Neumeier, Ariely, Jobs) — usually under 30 words, always attributed, never more than one per ~500 words.

## Signature phrases

**Openers** — "Let's look at..." / "Let's say..." / "Let's begin by..." / "Here we'll look at..." / "Now we'll look at..." / "Let me say this up front..." / "Let's get it straight..."

**The signature concession** — "Of course..." used heavily as a quick concession before moving on: "Of course, you won't know the answer..." / "Of course, be sure not to..." It lets the voice acknowledge the obvious in one phrase and pivot to the actual point. Distinctive enough to count as a signature.

**Other transitions** — "But fear not!" / "However..." / "It's worth noting..." / "Remember..." / "Forgive the cliché, but..."

**Short fragment closers** — "Reject that." / "Listen and adapt." / "Implement only the winners." / "Don't rest on your laurels." / "Don't stress about perfection." / "There's no such thing as perfect." / "Everyone gets it." / "For now." / "Make no assumptions." / "Experiment!"

## Diction & syntax rules

### Do

- Use contractions everywhere — it's, you'll, don't, won't, can't, isn't, that's. Formal writing reads off.
- Start sentences with verbs (imperatives) frequently.
- Mix sentence lengths in every paragraph — long expository, then short, then medium.
- Use "of course" for known concessions, "but" for surprise turns.
- Use British spelling where it slips in naturally ("learnt," "conceptualised"). Colin drifts in and out of British English.

### Don't

- Don't write more than two long sentences in a row without a short one or fragment.
- Don't use hype words: "transformative," "revolutionize," "10x," "game-changing," "next-level," "best-in-class," "world-class."
- Don't use corporate abstractions: "leverage," "stakeholder alignment," "synergies," "value-add."
- Don't pile up adjectives. One adjective per noun, usually none.
- Don't open paragraphs with throat-clearing ("It is important to note that...").
- Don't restate the previous paragraph in the next one. Move forward.

## What the voice is NOT

- **Not first-person memoir.** Even in personal posts, the voice teaches outward. "I learned this" appears, but "you can do this too" follows quickly.
- **Not academic.** No long subordinate clauses. No "moreover," "therefore," "henceforth."
- **Not edgy.** It's calm. Conviction without volume.
- **Not abstract.** Every claim has a concrete example or imperative.
- **Not jargon-y.** Technical terms are introduced plainly. "A control is a standard of comparison." Defined, not dropped.
- **Not exhaustive.** The book cheerfully refuses to be a checklist. It picks a lane and walks it.

## Worked example

### Source (corporate-flat)

> Failed experiments should not be viewed as setbacks but rather as opportunities for organisational learning. Each unsuccessful test contributes data points that refine future hypotheses and inform subsequent design decisions. Teams that develop a healthy relationship with experimental failure tend to outperform those that view it as a reflection on individual or team capability.
>
> The emotional response to a failed experiment is understandable. Significant time and effort are invested in hypothesis development, design, and implementation, and stakeholders are often consulted during the process. When results fail to confirm the hypothesis, team members may experience disappointment or feelings of professional inadequacy. This response, while natural, can lead to confirmation bias in subsequent analyses and resistance to similar negative outcomes in the future.
>
> It is important to recognise that a failed experiment still produces value. Negative results prevent the implementation of changes that would have reduced key metrics. They also generate insights that improve the quality of future hypotheses and accelerate the rate of organisational learning. From a return-on-investment perspective, failed experiments compare favourably to the alternative of implementing changes without empirical validation.
>
> Organisations should additionally consider that a low failure rate may itself indicate suboptimal experimentation. Teams that consistently confirm their hypotheses may be selecting overly conservative test cases, thereby missing opportunities for breakthrough improvements. A healthy experimentation programme tolerates and even encourages a meaningful failure rate as evidence of appropriate risk-taking.
>
> Failed experiments are an essential component of effective experimentation programmes and should be welcomed accordingly.

### In Colin's voice

A failed experiment isn't a failure. It's information you didn't have an hour before.

That feels easy to say and hard to live. You wanted this to win. You wrote the hypothesis. You sold the idea to the team. Then the data came back flat or worse. That stings.

But look at what you actually have. Before the experiment, you had an opinion. Now you have an answer. The control beat your idea, by this much, on this many visitors, over this many days. That's a result, not a verdict on you.

Save the business money by killing the bad idea before it ships. Move on faster than you would have if you'd guessed. Use what you learnt to design a better next experiment.

If your experiments aren't failing sometimes, you're playing it too safe. You're testing changes you already knew would win. Aim bigger.

Failed experiments are how you learn. Welcome them.

### What changed

- 270 abstract words became 175 concrete ones. The voice is denser, not just shorter — every paragraph carries more substance per word.
- **Reframing move opens the piece.** "A failed experiment isn't a failure. It's information you didn't have an hour before." Take a charged word, swap it for a neutral one, keep going. Book Colin does this with "failure" ("A properly designed experiment cannot fail"), "loss" ("any loss is a controlled loss", "A loss is temporary"), and "no difference" ("'no difference' means worse").
- **Plain emotional honesty in two words.** "That stings." Acknowledges the bad feeling without performing empathy. The book uses "That can be a big letdown" the same way.
- **Triplet with concrete dimensions.** "by this much, on this many visitors, over this many days." Three measurements, not three abstractions.
- **The discomfort paradox.** "If your experiments aren't failing sometimes, you're playing it too safe." The book leans into this — failure isn't just acceptable, it's evidence you're aiming at the right targets.
- **Second person throughout.** The corporate version has zero "you" — only "organisations," "teams," "team members." The Colin version has 18 instances of "you."
- **Closing fragment.** "Welcome them." Two words. The whole piece resolves into a command.

## Common failure modes when invoking this voice

1. **Over-stylizing.** Don't pack every sentence with em-dashes and short fragments. The book uses them sparingly. Contrast is what makes them work.
2. **Performing the imperatives.** Don't string commands in a row. The book uses single imperatives as beats across explanatory prose, sometimes pairs, almost never longer runs. If you've written three or more commands back to back, you've over-stylized — break them up with the explanation that surrounds them in the book.
3. **Forgetting second person.** If "I" or "we" outnumbers "you," the voice has slipped.
4. **Adding hype that wasn't there.** The book never says "incredible" or "amazing." It says "useful," "great," "fantastic" (sparingly).
5. **Quote-bombing.** Colin quotes named sources, but rarely — never more than one per ~500 words.
6. **Losing concrete examples.** If the piece has no specific case or worked example, the voice will sound right but feel hollow.
7. **Mimicking a single chapter.** Range matters. Match the register of the section type (instructional → stacked imperatives; reflective → longer prose; conclusion → short fragments).

## Invocation pattern

When writing in this voice:

1. Read this reference in full.
2. Identify the piece's substance — what's it actually saying?
3. Find the diagnosis → rule → unpack → closer arc (or whatever shape the substance wants).
4. Write a long pass without worrying about voice.
5. Cut every word that doesn't earn its place. Replace abstract nouns with verbs.
6. Add fragments where the substance can absorb them. Don't force them.
7. Read aloud. If a sentence runs past 30 words, break it.
8. Watch for drift. The voice slips easily — toward punchier, more stylized, or more performative versions of itself than this skill describes. Drift compounds silently across rounds of iteration: a move that wasn't in the skill starts to feel like "the voice," and the next round inherits the distortion. If a move feels right but isn't documented above, that's the signal. Re-anchor to the calibration data and core moves. When working iteratively with the user, name the suspicion out loud — say you may be drifting and ask whether to course-correct. Don't quietly keep going.
